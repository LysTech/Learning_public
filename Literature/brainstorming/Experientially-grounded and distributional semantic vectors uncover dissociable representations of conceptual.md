## Summary
This study compares experientially-grounded semantic vectors (based on sensorimotor and perceptual features) with distributional semantic vectors (derived from word co-occurrence statistics) to uncover distinct neural representations of conceptual categories. Using representational similarity analysis, the authors demonstrate that different semantic frameworks capture dissociable aspects of how the brain organizes conceptual knowledge.

## Key points
- Experientially-grounded and distributional semantic models reveal complementary rather than redundant representations of concepts
- Sensorimotor and perceptual features capture embodied aspects of conceptual organization in the brain
- Statistical word co-occurrence patterns represent different organizational principles than experiential grounding
- Representational similarity analysis reveals category-specific neural patterns that align with different semantic models
