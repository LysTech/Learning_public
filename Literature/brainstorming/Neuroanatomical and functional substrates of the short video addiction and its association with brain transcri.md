## Summary
This study investigates the neuroanatomical and functional brain substrates underlying short video addiction, examining associations between addictive behaviors and brain transcriptomic and cellular architecture. The research identifies structural and functional alterations in brain regions including the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) in individuals with problematic short video consumption.

## Key points
- Identifies neuroanatomical differences in individuals with short video addiction, including enlarged orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)
- Links addiction-related brain structural changes to transcriptomic and cellular-level architecture
- Provides mechanistic insights into how repeated consumption of short-form video content may alter brain development and function
- Suggests that OFC engagement and blood flow patterns during video consumption may contribute to addiction vulnerability
- Offers potential neurobiological targets for understanding and intervening in problematic short video use
